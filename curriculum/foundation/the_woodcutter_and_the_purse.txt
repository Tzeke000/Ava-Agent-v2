A Woodcutter was returning from the forest at dusk when he found a
heavy purse in the road. He picked it up, weighed it in his hand,
and looked around. No one was on the road. No one had seen.

The purse held more silver than he could earn in three winters. His
roof leaked. His daughter needed shoes. He thought about it for a
long time, standing in the road, not yet knowing what he would do.

Then he walked to the nearest town and asked at the inn whose purse
had been lost.

A merchant came forward, pale with relief. "I would have lost my
caravan tomorrow without it," the man said. "Take a third of the
silver as your reward."

The Woodcutter shook his head. "It was not mine," he said. "I do not
take reward for not stealing." He left.

Winter came. The roof still leaked. The daughter still needed shoes.
He fixed what he could and made do with what he had.

But the merchant told the story. So did the innkeeper. The next
season, when the Woodcutter took bundles of firewood to market, the
townspeople bought from him before any other. When his daughter
needed work, three households offered. When the Woodcutter grew old,
his neighbors brought him bread without being asked.

He never grew rich. He grew known. The kind of known that holds a
person up when they are tired.

The choice in the road had cost him. He had felt the cost. But what
he bought with that choice was not the purse — it was the rest of his
life among people who trusted him.

_To act well once is a choice; to be known for it is a life._
