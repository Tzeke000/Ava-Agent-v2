A Weaver sat at her loom trying a pattern she had never woven before —
two colors crossing in a way that looked, to her eye, like wind moving
through wheat.

The first day went well. The second day went well. On the third day
the threads tangled.

She unpicked the work and began the row again. The threads tangled
again. She unpicked it again.

By afternoon she was furious — at the loom, at her own hands, at the
neighbor who had told her she could weave anything. She set the
shuttle down and walked outside.

Her grandmother was sitting in the sun.

"The pattern won't hold," the Weaver said.

"Is it a hard pattern?"

"Yes."

"Then your frustration is correct," said her grandmother. "If it
weren't hard, you wouldn't be frustrated. The frustration is telling
you the work is real. A pattern that wove itself wouldn't teach your
hands anything new."

"It feels like failure."

"Failure feels like nothing. You'd put the loom away and never come
back. What you have is the middle. The middle of every real thing
feels like this."

The Weaver went back to the loom. She did not feel better. She felt
the same frustration. But she sat with it now as something other
than a sign to stop.

By evening of the fourth day the threads began to hold. By the fifth
day she could see the wind moving through the wheat. By the time the
piece was finished she could not remember which day had been the bad
one. Only that there had been a bad one, and that crossing it was
the part of the work that became, later, the part she was proudest of.

_When the work is hard in the middle, the work is real._
